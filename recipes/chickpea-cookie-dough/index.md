# Chickpea Cookie Dough

Edible cookie dough with 6g of fiber hiding in it.

Prep: 10 min · Cook: 0 min · Makes: about 1 1/2 cups (6 servings)

**Verdict: WORTH IT** — Nobody guesses the chickpeas. Nobody. The fibermaxxing girlies are right about this one.

_Tested 2026-07. Three full minutes in the processor and a hard chill are what separate 'surprisingly great' from 'hummus with chips.'_

## Ingredients

- 1 can (425g/15 oz) chickpeas, drained, rinsed hard, and patted dry
- 1/3 cup (85g) natural peanut butter (or almond/tahini)
- 3 Tbsp maple syrup
- 1 tsp vanilla extract
- 1/4 tsp Diamond Crystal kosher salt
- 2 Tbsp oat flour (or blitz oats) - only if needed to thicken
- 1/3 cup mini chocolate chips

## Steps

1. Rinse the chickpeas until the water runs clear, then rub them dry in a clean towel - this also sloughs off some skins. The drier the chickpeas, the less bean the dough tastes.
2. Food-process chickpeas, peanut butter, maple syrup, vanilla, and salt for a full 2-3 minutes, scraping down twice, until completely smooth. Under-processing is why people think this recipe is bad.
3. Too loose? Add oat flour a tablespoon at a time until it scoops like dough.
4. Pulse in the chocolate chips (or stir by hand to keep them whole).
5. Chill 30 minutes - cold is where it stops tasting healthy.
6. Eat with a spoon, stuff into dates, or roll into balls and keep in the fridge up to 5 days.

## Notes

- The math: a can of chickpeas carries roughly 20g fiber and 20g protein across the batch - about 6g fiber per serving with the peanut butter.
- This is dough, not dough-for-baking - no leavening, no egg; it stays dough.
- Natural peanut butter (just peanuts and salt) keeps it scoopable; the sweetened kind makes it stiff and cloying.

## When it goes wrong

- Tastes like hummus → under-processed or wet chickpeas; blitz longer, dry harder.
- Grainy → cheap blender or big-flake skins; peel more skins and process in a food processor, not a bullet.
- Too stiff → dry natural PB from the bottom of the jar; loosen with a teaspoon of milk.
- Too sweet → sweetened peanut butter stacking on the maple; use natural and re-taste before adding syrup.
